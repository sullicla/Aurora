The growth and maintenance of data infrastructures are closely tied to the environmental catastrophe, and earth extraction is a part of this process. Industries prioritize settler futures through extraction practices for metals, rare earths, and renewable energy sources.

Water is a crucial element for cooling the servers in data centers. Tech companies such as Google, Meta, and Microsoft are committing to becoming "water positive," but it remains unclear what that means in practice and who is accountable. The intersection of water and digital culture has become a subject of debate, especially in areas where water rights are contested.

Labor is another key to the data center industry, highlighting power dynamics and exploitation. The allure of technology supports the idea that data is progress, leading to a universalized human intelligence. But focusing on labor reveals the infrastructure and questions politics, ownership, and power.er.

Data centers consume 1-1.4% of the world's energy, but this number is evolving as data centers become major players in the energy industry. Companies invests in renewable energy, and some call data centers "virtual power plants" that can produce energy. Cryptocurrency mining is not included in the 1-1.4%, and Iceland's data centers were built for cryptocurrency mining. We need to focus on the evolving data/energy relationships to regulate data center energy consumption better.

Heat is at the center of digital data management and a losing battle data centers face. The US regulates them as thermal control and information processing entities, while climate change makes the Arctic crucial to data center development. Aesthetics link data centers with natural ecology in cold regions, revealing their limitations.
